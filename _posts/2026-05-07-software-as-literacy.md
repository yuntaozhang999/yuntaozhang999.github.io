On my commute today, I listened to a fascinating interview between **Andrej Karpathy** and **Boris Cherny** (co-founder of Anthropic and creator of Claude Code) hosted by Sequoia Capital. While the entire discussion was thought-provoking, one specific analogy shared by Boris resonated deeply with me: **software development as the new literacy.**

### The "Scribe" Era of Software

Boris framed our current state of software engineering through a historical lens. Before the invention of the printing press in the 15th century, literacy was a specialized, elite skill. Only a tiny fraction of the population—mostly scribes and the clergy—could read and write. Even kings and rulers often lacked these skills, relying entirely on professionals to handle their correspondence and record-keeping.

In Boris's view, we are currently in the **"Scribe Era" of software.** Today, if you want to manifest an idea into a functional application, you usually have to hire a "scribe"—a professional software engineer—who possesses the specialized knowledge of syntax, compilers, and deployment.

### The Printing Press Moment

The printing press didn't just make books cheaper; it fundamentally changed the world by making literacy universal. Within decades, reading and writing shifted from being a "professional service" to a "basic human skill." We no longer hire "professional writers" to send an email or write a report; we simply do it ourselves because we are literate.

Boris argues that AI is our generation's printing press for software. We are moving toward a world where "writing an app" will be as common and accessible as writing a text message or an email. 

### Mind-Blowing Statistics from the Interview

To illustrate how close we are to this reality, Boris shared some staggering personal metrics:
- **Zero Lines of Hand-Written Code:** Boris revealed that he has not written a single line of code by hand in 2026. Everything he ships is 100% model-generated.
- **The 150 PR Day:** He described a workflow using autonomous agents (like Claude Code) that allowed him to ship **150 pull requests in a single day**, often managing these agentic loops directly from his phone.
- **Model Interface Protocols (MCP):** He distinguished between traditional APIs—which he calls "Human Developer Interfaces"—and the new era of Model Context Protocols. In the future, software won't be built for humans to navigate; it will be built for models to interact with directly.

### "Vibe Coding" and the Karpathy Mindset

Andrej Karpathy, who spoke alongside Boris, complemented the discussion with his concept of **"Vibe Coding."** As implementation becomes a commodity, the human's role shifts to "steering" the model through high-level intent and "taste." Karpathy’s famous line, *"You can outsource your thinking, but you can't outsource your understanding,"* serves as a crucial reminder.

What makes Karpathy’s perspective so unique is his position at the absolute **forefront of the application layer.** Unlike many theorists, Karpathy is a builder who constantly ships side projects and explores the boundaries of what's possible. Two points from the interview (and his recent social media reflections) struck a deep chord:

1.  **AI Psychosis:** Karpathy described a state he calls "AI Psychosis"—an intense, almost anxious drive to constantly be "in the flow" with AI. It’s the feeling that if you aren't talking to a model, manifesting an idea, or building a product every single day, you are falling behind. I felt an immediate and powerful resonance with this. There is a specific kind of "progress anxiety" in this era: the need to constantly upgrade one’s AI usage, application skills, and mental models to stay relevant. It's not just about work; it's about the fear of losing touch with the most transformative tool of our time.

    This "builder energy" is contagious. In fact, my own experiment with **Voice Coding** was directly sparked by listening to Karpathy's interview on the *No Priors* podcast with Sarah Guo. Hearing them discuss the future of interaction models made me think: *"That sounds incredibly cool—I need to build that too."* It’s a perfect example of how Karpathy doesn't just explain the future; he dares you to go out and build it.

2.  **The Death of the "Niche App" (The MenuGen Lesson):** Karpathy shared a humbling realization regarding one of his own projects, **MenuGen**. He had spent significant effort building this specialized app, only to realize that as base models became more capable, the app itself became redundant. The model could now handle the entire logic, interface, and output directly. This goes beyond AI as a "speed boost" for coders; it’s a fundamental shift where the model *is* the application. We are moving toward a world where the "middlemen" apps—the ones that just wrap a model in a specific UI—will melt away, leaving only the user's intent and the model's capability.

Karpathy’s ability to take these complex, high-level shifts and explain them through the lens of a builder is why he remains such a vital voice. He makes the "expert" experience deeply relatable to anyone trying to navigate this transition.

### AI as the Digital Immune System: Jensen Huang's Perspective

Adding a complementary layer to this discussion about AI's role in society, I recently watched a CNBC interview where **Jensen Huang** (Nvidia CEO) spoke with Becky Quick. When asked about the potential for AI models to be weaponized, Jensen offered a brilliant and highly insightful analogy: **AI as white blood cells.**

He argued that the best defense against malicious AI is, in fact, more robust and capable AI. Just as our biological immune system uses specialized white blood cells to identify, attack, and neutralize pathogens, we will use "good AI" to monitor and defend our digital infrastructure against weaponized models. In this vision, AI isn't just a tool for creation or a potential threat; it becomes our primary defense mechanism—a scalable, digital immune system that evolves alongside the very threats it seeks to mitigate.

### Why This Matters

This shift suggests that the bottleneck for innovation will no longer be the ability to write code, but rather **domain expertise and taste.** 

Imagine a world where:
- A "really good accountant" builds their own bespoke accounting software because they understand the nuances of the tax code better than any engineer.
- A doctor creates a custom diagnostic tool tailored to their specific patient demographic without needing a technical team.
- Building an app becomes a "feature" of being a knowledgeable professional, rather than a separate career path.

### Final Thoughts

I found myself strongly agreeing with this perspective. As someone who works at the intersection of AI and software, I see the friction of implementation melting away every day. Coding is evolving from a craft of syntax into a craft of intent and orchestration. Whether it's Boris's "universal literacy" or Jensen's "digital immune system," it's clear that AI is moving from a specialized tool to a fundamental layer of our reality.

I plan to keep updating this blog as I explore this "post-literacy" world of software. The transition from scribes to universal literacy changed history; the transition from professional coding to universal app creation will likely do the same.
